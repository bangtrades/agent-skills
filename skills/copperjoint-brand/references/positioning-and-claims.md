## Claims Discipline (the FTC firewall)

The FTC's case against Tommie Copper was about *unsubstantiated claims that copper provided pain relief*. **CopperJoint must never make that mistake.** Here is the firewall:

### Claims we CAN make (substantiated by general compression literature)

✅ Graduated compression **is shown to** support venous return and circulation.
✅ Compression **may reduce** the feeling of lower-leg swelling during prolonged sitting or standing.
✅ Compression **is associated with** reduced muscle fatigue during high-impact activity.
✅ Compression **can help support** muscles and joints during movement.
✅ Copper-infused fabric **provides natural odor resistance** (this is well-established for copper as an antimicrobial — distinct from pain-relief claims).
✅ Many users **report** noticeable comfort during extended wear.
✅ Compression **is commonly used** for post-surgical swelling management under physician guidance.

### Claims we MUST NOT make

❌ Copper relieves pain. (This is what got Tommie Copper hit.)
❌ Compression cures arthritis / plantar fasciitis / sciatica.
❌ Wearing this product will heal your injury.
❌ Medical-grade / clinical-grade / FDA-approved (unless we actually have the regulatory clearance for that specific SKU — we generally do not).
❌ Better than [competitor X].
❌ Number one / #1 / best.

### Hedging vocabulary that's compliant

- "shown to" (when there's compression literature support)
- "is associated with"
- "many people experience"
- "supports" / "helps support"
- "may reduce" / "can help reduce"
- "designed to"
- "intended to support"

### Required disclaimer language (use on any health-adjacent content)

> Compression is a support tool, not a substitute for medical diagnosis. If you have a medical condition, please consult your physician before use.
